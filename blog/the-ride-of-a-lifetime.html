The ride of a lifetime

27/6/2026

Many years ago, I stumbled into a certain website, I found out about the IPhO, which looked kinda cool. There was no way I could have been able to know just how radically it would change my life. I learnt to work hard, very hard, to achieve an arbitrary goal. It’s hard to explain how proud and heartbroken I feel by the effort I made to get here. It's also hard for me to fathom the magnificence of meeting all the (probably close to a hundred) wonderful people I’ve met throughout this journey. But regardless, all journeys have to end at one point, and there’s no obligation for that ending to be a happy one.

I don’t think I’ve ever seen a more beautiful place than Bucaramanga in my entire life. My hopes were very high upon arrival, although it really didn’t feel like an IPhO to me until the opening ceremony for some odd reason. The first exam was going to be the experimental, for which I had as much fear as determination, maybe a very good day could compensate for my relative lack of preparation for it and I wouldn’t be doomed to trying to overturn a massive deficit on the theory exam. As usual, pre-exam chatter was centered about trying to uselessly guess what it would be about.

After two hours of waiting outside due to a delay caused by “logistical complications”, we were allowed into the exam room, and, to our surprise, saw only one lab instead of the usual two. It was a fluids one, and I had absolutely no experience with anything like it. I wanted to curse my luck, but I somehow managed to retain my focus, and after some good minutes of confusion and aimlessness I was able to lock in and drop a barely decent exam of which I am extremely proud of.

But everything went downhill after time was up. I had noticed that a guy besides me was having trouble with his kit, he was regularly asking for assistance with it, and at the 5 hour mark, a now trauma-inducing voice announced that in the spirit of fairness, all those who had failing experimental kits would go rest, have lunch, and then get an extra two and a half hours to finish the exam. After some minutes of confusion, many of us were allowed to leave our seats, and hell broke loose. Everyone was shouting and discussing stuff outside, teammates were angry with some of their peers, and from that moment everyone knew that Colombia 2026 would inevitably go on to be known as one of the greatest disasters in the history of the International Physics Olympiads. Some even feared it would end up being worse than the infamous 2017 Indonesia edition.

Everyone had conflicting information, some of us were told that those with failing kits would be forced to redo the entire thing from scratch, others were told that they would be allowed to continue working, some complained that they were robbed of more than 2.5 hours by the inconveniences, some complained that 2.5 hours was an absurdly long amount of time to give, some noted that the “isolation” of students was executed in an astoundingly poor way and some of those with failing equipment got out of their seats and talked with others who did finish the experiment, which would make allowing them to retake the whole thing scandalous, but some of us were also unsure if everything had actually worked perfectly because at many moments liquids leaked and good-looking data ended up giving inaccurate but not very uncertain results. Some said that many of the reported kit failures were the students’ fault, others said that the exam taking centre did not have enough electrical power to supply all 430 kits, being the reason why so many failed, but many reported blatant cases of incompetence in the manufacturing of their equipment, and the quality of the technical support everyone received during the exam varied wildly solely depending on how lucky you got with the engineer that would be called up to solve your issue. It got bad enough for rumours to go around accusing the engineers assigned to help the Chinese team of being racist and unhelpful, leading the most dominant entity in the competition’s history to be on the brink of missing out on 5 golds for two years in a row for the first time since 2001-2002, potentially having their worst result of the 21st century.

When most of the dust was settled, the overworked guides ordered over one hundred students to stay, and the rest of us, those who had reported a working setup, boarded the buses back to our respective hotels. Notably, many had initially left their cubicles and isolation because even though their equipment had failed in some part, they didn’t understand what on earth was going on, and then decided that they would rather redo the entire thing. The hotels housed record-breaking cortisol levels, as all of us there feared that those who were staying and had broken their isolation were going to get 50% more time to complete the exam. Imagine our surprise when, after being confined to a single floor for one hour and a half, we saw our peers arrive without having been allowed to touch the exam again.

Disarray was such that some people ended up having lunch at 7 pm, none of us knew what the international board was doing. We guessed that the experimental exam would have to be cancelled, but that would mark an embarrassing first for IPhO and experimentalists would be screwed over, we didn’t know how many would be down for such an open admission of failure. Team leaders could also refuse to do anything, irreversibly screw over hundreds of students who had spent thousands of hours to get there, which would probably get them shamed by the rest of the olympiad community, yet people without context would probably just look at the results and think “Huh, that must have been a tricky experimental” before moving on. Way more medals could also be given out on a basis of score uncertainty or some other mechanism, but that would probably come with the removal of rankings, like what happened in 2017, and the complete devaluation of every single medal. There was no hope for fairness anymore, and the first IPhO in South America was ruined.

The “rest” day’s excursion was filled with stress and speculation, I was in the peculiar position of feeling angry at the entire mess, feeling sad for my friends, and acknowledging that my long-standing fear of ruining my last IPhO’s result on the first exam had been defeated. The theory exam was what I had poured thousands of hours preparing for, and it was likely going to be the only one that mattered. After a stressful, angry, and nice day I went to sleep before what I thought would be the most important day of my life.

I always knew that the mindset I carried into the exams was going to be as, if not more, important as the extra preparation I could get in the month leading up to them. Having a pretty good number of personal experiences in olympiads to look back into, I had decided that what worked best for me was to think of the whole thing as “just another day in the office”. I knew that if I performed as well as in the typical simulation, I would do really well. But on the morning of July 8th, I got greedy. Everything up to that point had been raising my odds, yet for some reason I decided that I’d actually try to get an adrenaline spike and pull out the “lock in of a lifetime” whatever that meant. The idea of giving it all out for one last physics olympiad exam was too appealing, and the possibility of approaching the most important 5 hours of my life so far with a good mindset was gone.

There were no delays, I opened the paper, saw it was short, and I didn’t know what to feel. My brain was deep enough into speculation mode after what had happened over the previous 48 hours that it’d always try to make judgments about any new piece of information I got in order to estimate how it affected my expected result. T1, the first of the three problems, was three shorter, standard problems. T3 was a very nice thermodynamics problem about magnetic cooling. T2 was geometrical optics. The first one in “modern” IPhOs. I had, for some reason, not taken geo optics preparation seriously, unlike every single other topic that could potentially show up. My greedy mood made me want to solve it anyways. Turns out I’m pretty bad at geometry for some reason, I wasted time, I tried to rush the other two problems, I made way too many stupid mistakes, and despite attempting 85% of the exam, I got out of the examination room, and I was wondering whether I was going to reach 50% of the total score. Somehow, after years and thousands of hours of preparation, I had sabotaged myself in the most crucial hours, after which there was nothing I could do. My hope shattered into a million pieces.

Team leaders were waiting for us outside the examination room. The first one I stumbled into was Jaan Kalda. I asked him what he thought about this year’s theory exam. He said it was slightly easier than 2025’s but better quality, an opinion shared by most people. Later, I found my team leader and he tried to explain what was actually going on. As it turns out, the experimental exam hadn’t actually been cancelled. The international board’s idea was to separate students into two leaderboards, theory only and theory+experiment, put all those who reported working kits into the T+E category, give those who reported failing kits an option to choose what category they wanted to be in, and then assign medals with the traditional percentage cutoffs to each leaderboard. This enraged many, as the T category was looking like it would turn out to be extremely competitive, because many strong countries had their participants report a broken kit at one time or another and they would rather only have their very strong theory exam count. (It looks like almost all of the exam was actually quite standard, and many contestants had done very similar problems at some point in their training camps). Under this scheme, most people told me that my ~25 total points would almost certainly be a silver medal in the T+E category, and that yeah, I had fucked up a very good chance to get gold, but I shouldn’t stress much about it because this year was scuffed and silver would still be a great achievement. But the range of the speculated silver cutoff overlapped with the estimated range of my score, and anxiety started eating me alive.

Furthermore, there was no final decision on the medal-assigning mechanism and team leaders were still going to have a vote. Many proposals were made. One suggested that there should be a single leaderboard with all scores, inserting the theory only ones scaled up by 5/3. The winning proposal was that of Jaan Kalda, it was a modification of the two leaderboards one, but here everyone would be given a baseline medal according to their theory score, and then a T+E leaderboard would be constructed for those with working experiments, T+E cutoffs would be determined traditionally for that leaderboard, and if their T+E score gave them a better medal their result would be updated to that. However, the proposal would clearly result in handing out way more medals than usual. And some leaders started complaining that a big chunk of the students labeled with working experiments actually had partly malfunctioning setups. Therefore, using some obscure algorithm that as of July 14th 2026 nobody seems to understand, all experimentals with a score <14 were deemed potentially broken, and taken out of the T+E population, leaving ridiculously high cutoffs for all medals there. Everyone can clearly see how the final T+E cutoffs don't make sense at all, as the gap between them and T cutoffs was 15 points for HM, 14 for bronze, 12 for silver, and 14 for gold. No HM-level student was getting 15 points in the experiment. That is an absurd distribution, experimental scores are correlated with theory ones and by only taking the sample of those good enough to score 14 points you make the contestant population in that category way stronger. I don’t have a lot of data, but I know that some gold medalists had ~12 or 13 points with working experiments, and I have only talked with two people who reported a score over 14 to me, I personally don’t know of anyone who had their experiment improve their medal with this rule, I’d wager that almost all of the extra medals this year were thanks to the very deplorable pre-moderation grading causing moderation to give astronomical score increases to many. I honestly don’t know what the point of the implemented solution was, just remove the experiment at that point and it will be effectively the same but with way less ambiguity. After talking with my team leader and lots of fellow contestants, I can’t help but feel that nobody actually knows what went on and why we ended up with a situation like this. [Maybe the picture will be clearer in some days, I’ll make sure to update this post if that is the case]

While all of these discussions were going on, my brain was constantly speculating about what was going to happen. I knew I had completely fucked up gold, and I knew that my theory score was quite bad. But many 2025 bronze medalists had actually achieved a similar score to me, and surely this year’s paper wasn’t that much easier than 2025, a year in which low-silver medalists got about 14 or 15 points in theory and the cutoff was 22.9. Maybe I could actually get something out of this catastrophe.

After having to suffer through three conferences alone, without internet, I got word of the rumoured cutoffs. Everyone was freaking out. 21 points for a honourable mention in T+E. We didn’t know how that could even be real, information was sparse and contradictory, our team leaders didn’t want to leak anything to us, but mine told me that he would actually have to fight for bronze after markers had done a terrible job with my paper (Probably because I was the first one graded, and they were presumably trying to look strict), and that statement would only make sense with the rumoured T+E values that came from an excel screenshot being passed around (21 HM, 24.75 bronze, 29 silver, 39 gold). The real silver and gold cutoffs ended up being slightly higher than those in the “leaked” screenshot (21, 24.75, 31, and 40.6). I was finished, and after all that horrible amount of speculation, which I wish I’ll never have to deal with again in my entire life, I knew I was getting bronze for a second year in a row, I was not going to get a better result despite pouring in thousands of hours of extra hard work.

My team leader did a great job in moderation, recovering most of the points that poor grading had taken away from me, Others weren't as lucky, Patrick Wang from Canada was officially robbed of a bronze medal in moderation, the IPhO board agreed with his appeal, but they said they just couldn't give out more medals after the closing ceremony. Some people said grading was lenient for them, but it looks like it was very poor quality in most cases. Regardless, most team leaders, i.e. those who actually know most, think that the final outcome was one of the least unfair ones realistically possible, and I sort of agree. Fumbling the theory exam was entirely my fault, some people got very unfairly treated by this solution, but there was no way to be fair. Any medal-inflating solution would be bad for those who would've gotten the same medal in a normal year. The communication should have been way better, but I'm glad that team leaders were able to find a decent way out of the whole conundrum, despite also regretting that the last change they made to it probably dropped me to a lower medal.

I've spent most of this post complaining and describing the insanity that went on in a random Colombian city during the first half of July 2026. But there was also another side to the whole experience. I don't think I've ever had as much fun in my life, I suffered, I cried, and I also absolutely loved it. I’m very thankful for meeting all the new friends I made, and I’m aware of how crazy this whole thing was regardless of the way it turned out. The support so many gave me after being extremely frustrated by the result was also extremely heartwarming. To everyone who was there when I needed it most, thank you, thank you for making this insane experience bearable. And as I wrote some very long weeks ago

“Life will go on after the exams, whether the result is euphoric or not”

I’ll always know I gave it my all, now I’m free, and there’s still a lot to do.
